Galloitalic colonies

Gallo-Italic minorities of Sicily

Traditionally, in the absence of direct historical sources, the hypothetical homeland of the settlers has been recognised as mainly Monferrato, between southern Piedmont and northern Liguria, but also, further south, as a large part of the Savona hinterland and, to the east, as the Lombard Cispadane wedge and part of the Piacenza area (cf. Petracco Sicardi 1969: 356-357), without, however, completely ruling out further contributions from north-western Italy.

Only the localities of Group A are communities characterised by “dialectal bilingualism” (Trovato 2013), i.e. communities in which, at least until the turn of the Millennium, the Gallo-Italic variety has remained distinct from the “local Sicilian”, a form of Sicilian that is interfered with by Gallo-Italic elements, especially at the phonetic level. In the remaining localities (Group B) there is a “dialectal monolingualism”, in that there is only one dialectal variety, ranging from a Sicilianised Gallo-Italic dialect to a Sicilian Gallo-Italicised dialect. Since around the 1970s, the repertoire of all Gallo-Italic communities has also included (regional) Italian.

These dialects can be aggregated in 4 micro-areas on the basis of their mutual affinities: 1) the area of Sanfratellano, spoken in San Fratello and, identically, in Acquedolci; 2) the Novarese area, which includes the hamlets and scattered villages of the current municipalities of Novara of Sicily and Fondachelli-Fantina; 3) the Nicosiano area, which includes Nicosiano and Sperlinghese, two very similar dialects, the latter being the rustic variant of the former, since it originated from the repopulation of Sperlinga by Nicosia families in the 16th century; 4) the area of Aidonese and Piazzese, two dialects that have much in common from a structural point of view (Aidone was originally an offshoot of Piazza Armerina).
